Product Ownership

R&D Digital Transformation

How I became the de facto product owner for a multi-site lab digitization — without the title, the authority, or the playbook.

The Business Problem

A multi-site chemical company needed to replace physical lab notebooks and fragmented Excel workflows with a centralized digital platform. The goal: 30% reduction in formulation-to-production time, data sharing across 3 facilities, and 20+ years of historical data made searchable.

The catch: 12 scientists across 3 facilities, each with their own way of working, their own Excel setups, their own reasons not to change. We did not have a PM at the time, so every product decision landed on me.

Key Product Decisions

Decision 1: Template Unification vs. Customization

Every scientist wanted their own template. I had to choose between giving everyone exactly what they wanted (expensive, unmaintainable) and creating a unified framework that was flexible enough to feel personal but standardized enough to enable cross-site data sharing.

I chose a configurable template framework. Standardized structure where field visibility, naming, and layout could adapt per user while keeping the underlying data model consistent. Scientists got personalization; leadership got comparable data across sites.

Decision 2: Scope Management Under Pressure

The wish list grew constantly. I made the call to scope Phase 1 tightly around core workflows and defer advanced analytics to Phase 2, prioritizing adoption speed over feature completeness.

Trade-off: Some user frustration with missing features balanced against on-time delivery and budget compliance. Better to ship something working than delay for nice-to-haves.

Decision 3: Strategic Stakeholder Sequencing

I always validated decisions with management before presenting to lab personnel. This ensured solutions met business requirements first, then I could advocate confidently for user adoption. It prevented rework and maintained trust with both groups.

Execution: Coordinating 7 Teams

I ran point across lab scientists (workflow design, training), executives (requirements, dashboards), the production plant (formulation handoff), IT (ERP integration, SSO), our internal product team (feature pitches), finance (contracts, budget), and development (bugs, testing).

My coordination practices: solution architecture map with C-suite buy-in before building anything, RACI chart to prevent dropped tasks, feedback loops during rollout, and on-site support for the first 3 months.

Change Management

Turning a Near-Failure Into a Growth Engine

From escalation to 3× expansion — how leaning into negative feedback saved an account and improved the platform for everyone.

The Crisis

Six weeks after deploying at a multi-site chemical company (30 users, 4 departments, 3 locations), the account was in full escalation. Leadership couldn't extract the reports they expected. Scientists found the interface unituitive. Nearly everyone resisted. Except one scientist who showed genuine engagement.

The customer's message was clear: "This isn't working. We're considering pulling the plug."

The Feedback Triage Framework

Instead of getting defensive, I leaned into the negativity. I created a comprehensive feedback log, everything from articulate technical complaints to vague frustrations like "What is this? I don't understand this!"

Then I classified every item into three categories and resolved them in deliberate sequence:

CategoryWhat It MeansResolution Order
Training gapUsers didn't know how to do something the platform already supportedFirst — shrinks the complaint list fast
Implementation issueSomething I could fix through configuration or workflow adjustmentSecond — quick wins that rebuild trust
Product gapGenuine platform limitation requiring developmentLast — documented with full business context

Why this order matters: Many "product problems" are actually knowledge gaps. By resolving training issues first, the complaint list shrank significantly and users' confidence grew. By the time I escalated genuine product gaps to engineering, I had a clean, validated list, not a mess of mixed signals.

The Champion Strategy

That one engaged scientist became my secret weapon. I invested extra time with her, making her a power user. She started helping colleagues voluntarily, demonstrating workflows in her own words and context. Peer advocacy moved the needle where my training sessions couldn't.

Product Impact

The genuine product gaps I escalated drove platform-wide improvements: a side-by-side experiment comparison feature (replacing painful scrolling), streamlined navigation with clearer data hierarchy, and shortcut buttons that reduced click paths for common tasks. These shipped for all customers, not just this one.

Product Discovery

From Field Signals to Product Roadmap

How a Solutions Engineer built a continuous discovery engine that shaped a SaaS platform.

The Context

At a vertical B2B SaaS company serving the chemical industry, my role as Solutions Engineer put me closer to real users. I participated in weekly triage meetings with the product team, reviewed the full ticket backlog, and accompanied product managers on every user interview.

Over time, this proximity became a strategic advantage. The CTO said it best: "When she says 'I need,' it always means a customer needs it."

The Process

I didn't file one-off feature requests. I ran a systematic validation process: observe a pain point during implementation, validate it across multiple customers, frame it in product-and-engineering terms, then present it with business context and specific customer use-cases. The result was that my requests had an unusually high implementation rate, because they arrived pre-validated and well-framed.

Feature Spotlight: Hidden Fields

The platform had binary field visibility: visible or not visible. But "not visible" excluded fields from reports and exports entirely. Customers were forced to choose between cluttered interfaces and incomplete data.

I identified that users needed a third state: present in the data layer, absent from the UI. This architectural distinction unlocked entirely new configuration templates and eliminated a painful tradeoff for every customer.

Feature Spotlight: Custom Action Buttons

The platform supported automated actions, but only at hardcoded system events. I didn't ask for a workaround for one customer, but for extensibility: let users define their own trigger points. Once shipped, custom action buttons became one of the platform's most versatile tools, enabling customer-specific workflows without engineering intervention.

The Bigger Play: Privileges Redesign

I also identified the need for a comprehensive privileges system overhaul. Rather than pushing it as a single massive request, I decomposed it into smaller, independently valuable tickets, then discussed the overall vision with the CTO. The feature was scoped, validated, and parked for the right moment. Not every feature needs to ship immediately.

Systems Thinking

The Post Validator

A pet project about converting tacit knowledge into structured criteria, even when nobody's paying me to.

The Situation

I volunteered to help a skills academy for young learners with their marketing operations. The owner runs the school, and a junior marketing manager handles social media. When I joined their planning meetings, the content process was entirely conversational - no structure, no pipeline, no shared system.

So I built one! A spreadsheet organizing all planned posts by course, post type, and phase, with priorities and dates. The meetings immediately became more productive.

The Bottleneck

But a pattern emerged. Week after week, posts would get stuck in the "validation" phase, waiting for the owner to review colors, fonts, formatting, tone. Three posts per week piling up, sitting for weeks. The content shifted from proactive to reactive, and opportunities were missed when viral trends passed them by.

The root cause wasn't that the owner was a bottleneck by choice. She was too busy running a school. The real problem was deeper: her quality standards existed only in her head. The marketing manager couldn't self-validate because the criteria had never been made explicit.

The Solution: Codifying Tacit Knowledge

I'm building a web application where the owner can define validation requirements in a structured format (enforcing how each requirement is shaped), set priorities, and the marketing manager can upload posts and evaluate them against those standards. Requirements like "the title must be white," "all colors must be brand colors," "the logo must be visible on at least one slide."

Phase 1 covers technical/visual validation. The architecture supports expanding to tone and messaging analysis in later phases.

The PM Discipline

This is a pet project, but I'm treating it like a real product: I wrote a comprehensive Software Requirements Specification with data models, acceptance criteria, and risk mitigation. I defined success metrics (80% adoption, 20% score improvement, 30% reduction in review time). I scoped an MVP and parked Phase 2 features separately.

The risk table includes "Low adoption by marketing manager" as high-impact/medium-probability, with the mitigation being to involve her in the design process.
